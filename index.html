About Me

I'm a PhD candidate in mechanical engineering at the University of Colorado at Boulder. My job is to make air quality measurements affordable, which will improve our understanding of air pollution sources and sinks, as well as personal exposure to poor air quality. My work is a full-stack engineering project, and includes computational fluid dynamics modeling, mechanical design and construction, electrical construction and debugging, several multiple-week field campaigns, and data analysis. I'm particularly excited about data, and lately, I've been exploring how machine learning can improve the performance of the low-cost sensors in our instruments. In a few short months, I have been able to significantly improve the regression models that we use to calibrate our sensors, and therefore greatly improve the accuracy of our measurements- all without spending any time improving instrument design or performing more experiments. The potential to extract information from the mountains of data that have already been collected is astonishing, and I am eager to explore these hidden possibilities. When I'm not developing air quality instruments or playing with data, I enjoy educating K-12 students about engineering, mentoring teenage girls, skiing, rock climbing, biking, yoga, eating at the amazing restaurants of San Francisco, and spending time with family and friends.
