Air Quality Research

developing low-cost instrumentation

When people think about air quality, they typically wonder how much pollution they inhale. They may be concerned about how poor air quality affects their health, and they should be. The World Health Organization estimates that poor indoor air quality is responsible for 2 million premature deaths per year. While most of these deaths occur in developing nations, even those living in high-income countries are impacted by pollution. 200,000 annual premature deaths can be attributed to urban air pollution in the wealthiest nations alone.

Given that poor air quality is a critical public health issue, one might assume that air quality measurements are focused on personal exposure to pollutants. Unfortunately, this is not the case. Pollutants are emitted everywhere, and sources are diverse. Due to this variability, personal exposure is highly dependent on location. The air quality in one's home is different than in her car, at her workplace, or at her local pub. To accurately measure personal exposure, researchers need measurements of air quality everywhere people spend time. Why don't we do this? Because measurements are too expensive.

Enter the Hannigan Lab. We use low-cost gas phase sensors to to develop inexpensive monitoring systems. While existing measurement schemes range in price from thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars, our monitors can be produced for just a few hundred bucks. We have used this technology to monitor everything from indoor air quality in rural Ghana to ozone flux to vegetation in Colorado. Since our pods are so affordable, we have been able to achieve much greater spatial coverage than is typically possible.

While working on my PhD, I used the sensor technology mentioned above to develop low-cost dynamic flux chambers that measure ozone and carbon dioxide deposition to vegetation. I validated the performance of my chambers and used the them to measure spatial variability in fluxes on the front range of Colorado. The scope of my work included computational fluid dynamics modeling, mechanical design and construction, electrical construction and debugging, several multiple-week field campaigns, and data analysis. Currently, I'm exploring how machine learning can improve sensor performance. Check out the data page for more information on my sensor optimization work.
